    How to Choose a Doula in Metro Detroit¶

    Finding the right doula shouldn’t feel like a scavenger hunt. This guide gives you a simple, culturally-rooted process to locate and vet Black doulas across Metro Detroit.

    Shortcut: Browse the community-vetted directory: Find Black Doulas →

    What a Doula Does (and Doesn’t)¶

    • Birth doula: Prepares you for labor, supports you during birth, advocates for your preferences.
    • Postpartum doula: Supports recovery, newborn care, feeding, sleep, and household transitions.
    • They don’t replace medical care. Doulas provide emotional, informational, and physical support—not clinical care.

    Step-by-Step: Find + Vet Your Doula¶

    1. Search locally
      Use filters for city/ZIP, virtual vs. in-person, languages, and insurance/Medicaid/self-pay.
    2. Check experience fit
      Settings (hospital, center, home), specialties (VBAC, multiples, high-risk, trauma-informed).
    3. Cultural concordance matters
      Prioritize doulas embedded in Detroit’s Black birthwork community.
    4. Interview 2–3 doulas (30–45 min each)
      Compare notes right after.

    Interview Questions (Copy/Paste)¶

    • Availability & backup coverage for your due window
    • Support approach in hospital settings; advocacy style
    • Local relationships with L&D units and midwifery practices
    • Inclusion practices; experience with diverse family structures
    • Lactation support scope & referrals
    • What’s included vs. add-ons; emergency scenarios; fees/sliding scale

    Budgeting & Payment¶

    • Typical packages: prenatals + birth + 1–2 postpartum visits.
    • Ask about sliding scale, payment plans, and any community funds.
    • Insurance/Medicaid pilots exist—confirm in writing.

    Tip: Popular doulas book 2–4 months ahead.

    Red Flags¶

    • Outcome guarantees
    • Dismissive of your preferences/culture
    • No written agreement or unclear backup

    Preparing to Work Together¶

    • Birth preferences worksheet
    • Support team roles (partner, doula, family)
    • Hospital policies (visitors, induction/C-section protocols)

    Postpartum & Lactation Support (Metro Detroit)¶

    Use this guide to find local lactation help, postpartum doulas, and community resources rooted in the needs of Black families.

    Need a provider now?
    - Postpartum Doulas
    - Lactation Consultants / IBCLC

    The Postpartum Plan (Quick Template)¶

    • Primary support: partner/family + doula (roles + schedules)
    • Feeding plan: chest/breast, bottle, combo; who to call for latch issues
    • Meals/chores: meal train, groceries, laundry plan
    • Sleep: shifts, safe sleep setup
    • Appointments: pediatric, OB/midwife, lactation follow-ups
    • Mind: mood check-ins, anxiety, birth processing

    Print this section and keep a copy on the fridge.

    Lactation: When to Get Help¶

    • Painful latch beyond the first minute
    • Cracked/bleeding nipples or mastitis symptoms
    • Low output/weight concerns or <6 wet diapers/day after day 5
    • Pumping/return-to-work planning

    Who to call: IBCLC (clinical), CLC/CLE (education), peer counselors (community).

    Mental Health & Emotional Recovery¶

    It’s normal to feel overwhelmed. Seek help for persistent sadness, anxiety, panic, or intrusive thoughts. Ask about perinatal mental health clinicians and group support.

    Practical Setups That Help¶

    • Nourish corner, diaper stations, night shift bin, visitor policy (“helpers do chores first”).

    • Postpartum doulas • Lactation (IBCLC/CLC) • Pelvic floor therapy • Parent groups • Feeding supplies

    Funding & Benefits for Black Families (Michigan)¶

    This page is a starting map to programs Michigan families often use during pregnancy, postpartum, and early childhood. Always confirm details on official sites.

    Navigation tip: Start applications on the official Michigan benefits portal. Keep IDs, pay stubs, lease, and birth records ready.

    Core Programs to Explore¶

    • Health coverage (pregnancy & baby) – Medicaid options for pregnant people and infants/children
    • Food & nutrition – WIC and related nutrition support
    • Cash/basic needs – cash assistance, utility help, housing support (income/situation dependent)
    • Child care assistance – subsidies to reduce daycare/in-home care costs
    • Early learning – Head Start/Early Head Start and state preschool
    • Home visiting – pregnancy–age 3 family support programs

    Log application dates, case numbers, and contacts.

    Tips for a Smoother Application¶

    Create a document kit, report changes fast, appeal if denied, and request interpreters/accommodations if needed.

    Community & Bridge Supports¶

    Ask doulas about community funds/sliding scale; look for diaper banks, housing/utility aid, and ride programs for appointments.
