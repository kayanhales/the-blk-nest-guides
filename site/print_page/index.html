    Why This Guide Has a Black Lens¶

    Black parents face unique realities, medically, culturally, and socially.

    We are more likely to:

    • Have our pain minimized
    • Receive less information about available care
    • Be expected to “push through” instead of being supported
    • Be praised for strength instead of offered rest

    This guide centers Black experiences without centering fear.

    It highlights care paths, language, and resources that many of us only discover after struggling, often because someone else finally told us they existed.

    We believe information should not be gatekept.

    What This Guide Is, and Is Not¶

    This guide is:

    • Honest
    • Calm
    • Practical
    • Grounded in lived experience
    • Meant to be revisited as your needs change

    This guide is not:

    • Medical advice
    • A replacement for professional care
    • A checklist you must complete
    • A judgment of how you parent

    A Gentle Reality Check¶

    Parenthood, especially motherhood, is beautiful.
    Parenthood, especially motherhood, is also hard.

    Both can be true — especially for Black parents.

    This guide exists because many of us enter pregnancy and parenthood with love, faith, and excitement, but without clear information about what life can actually feel like on the other side.

    This is not meant to scare you.
    It’s meant to ground you.

    Support & Community

    Community Spaces¶

    Community spaces offer support that appointments often don’t.

    These are places designed for connection, not rushing or fixing.

    What Community Spaces Provide¶

    Many community spaces offer: - Emotional support - Shared experience - Education - Gentle accountability - Relief from isolation

    They are especially important during pregnancy and postpartum, when support often drops off.

    Community Spaces to Know About¶

    Fourth Tri Sanctuary¶

    Focus: Postpartum support

    Fourth Tri centers the often-overlooked postpartum period and provides: - Postpartum education - Support spaces - Community connection

    This is a key resource for the “after birth” phase.

    Website: Fourth Tri Sanctuary

    Honey for Moms¶

    Focus: Black maternal wellness

    Honey for Moms creates intentional space for Black mothers to: - Connect - Rest - Speak honestly - Feel less alone

    This is community-centered care.

    Website: Honey. For Moms

    Nature’s Playhouse¶

    Focus: Families, children, and connection

    Nature’s Playhouse offers: - Family-centered spaces - Play-based connection - Community building alongside children

    Support does not have to happen without your kids.

    Website: Natures PlayHouse

    How to Show Up and Ask for Help¶

    If you are overwhelmed, exhausted, or stretched thin, you do not need a mindset shift. You need support you can actually access.

    This page is about how to ask — not why you should.

    Step 1: Identify What Kind of Help You Need¶

    You don’t need perfect clarity. Pick the closest match.

    Emotional support

    • Someone to listen
    • A support group
    • A therapist
    • A postpartum support space

    Physical support

    • Help with the baby
    • Help overnight
    • Help with meals
    • Help with household tasks

    Information support

    • Understanding postpartum symptoms
    • Learning about available services
    • Help navigating benefits or care options

    If everything feels overwhelming, start with emotional support.

    Step 2: Use Short, Direct Language¶

    You do not need to explain your whole situation.

    Use one sentence.

    Examples you can copy:¶

    • “I’m overwhelmed and need support.”
    • “I’m not okay and need help finding resources.”
    • “Do you know any postpartum support groups?”
    • “I need help during the day / overnight.”
    • “Can you help me figure out next steps?”

    That is enough information to start.

    Step 3: Decide Where to Ask¶

    Choose one place. Not all of them.

    For emotional support

    • Postpartum support groups
    • Community spaces
    • A therapist
    • A trusted friend

    For physical help

    • Partner
    • Family member
    • Postpartum doula
    • Night support / newborn doula

    For guidance

    • Community organizations
    • Lactation consultants
    • Postpartum care providers
    • WIC or local maternal health orgs

    If one option doesn’t work, move to the next.

    Step 4: Lower the Bar for Showing Up¶

    You do NOT need to:

    • Be prepared
    • Share your story
    • Speak the whole time
    • Stay the full session

    You CAN:

    • Join late
    • Leave early
    • Listen only
    • Say “I’m just here today”

    Showing up once counts.

    Step 5: If You’re Asking Someone You Know¶

    Be specific. Vague requests often don’t get met.

    Instead of:

    • “I need help”

    Try:

    • “Can you watch the baby for one hour?”
    • “Can you bring a meal this week?”
    • “Can you sit with me while I rest?”
    • “Can you help me find postpartum resources?”

    Specific asks are easier to respond to.

    Step 6: If Help Feels Hard to Ask For¶

    This usually means:

    • You’ve been the strong one
    • You’re used to managing alone
    • You don’t want to be a burden

    None of that means you don’t need help. It means you’ve been carrying too much.

    Step 7: Set Boundaries Early¶

    Getting help does NOT mean:

    • Taking advice you didn’t ask for
    • Staying in spaces that feel unsafe
    • Explaining your choices repeatedly

    You are allowed to say:

    • “That’s not helpful for me.”
    • “I’m not looking for advice right now.”
    • “This space isn’t a good fit.”

    You can leave and try something else.

    If You Don’t Know Where to Start¶

    Start here:

    • One support group
    • One community space
    • One conversation

    Support is not something you earn. It is something you access.

    That is the goal.

    Care & Healing

    Pelvic Floor Therapy¶

    Pelvic floor therapy helps with recovery after pregnancy and birth. It is not only for people who had complications.

    Many postpartum symptoms are common — but not something you have to live with.

    What Pelvic Floor Therapy Helps With¶

    Pelvic floor therapy can help with:

    • Pain during or after sex
    • Leaking urine or stool
    • Pelvic pain or pressure
    • Core weakness or separation (diastasis recti)
    • Pain after vaginal birth or C-section
    • Feeling “not connected” to your core

    When to Consider Pelvic Floor Therapy¶

    Consider pelvic floor therapy if:

    • You’re postpartum and still uncomfortable
    • You were never assessed after birth
    • You were told symptoms were “normal” but they persist
    • You want support returning to movement or exercise

    How to Find a Pelvic Floor Therapist¶

    Start here:

    • APTA Pelvic Health Physical Therapist Directory

    Search tips:

    • Look for “pelvic health” or “women’s health”
    • Ask if they treat postpartum and post-C-section patients
    • Ask if they have experience with postpartum care

    Black Pelvic Floor Therapists (Metro Detroit)¶

    If you are looking for a Black pelvic floor therapist in Metro Detroit:

    Pelvic Noire
    Founded by Dr. Kamaria Washington, Pelvic Noire centers pelvic health care for Black women and birthing people, with a strong focus on postpartum recovery and education.
    https://www.pelvicnoire.com/

    Insurance & Referrals¶

    • Some providers require a referral; some do not
    • Call your insurance and ask about “pelvic floor physical therapy”
    • Ask your OB or midwife directly for a referral if needed

    Lactation Support¶

    Lactation support is help with feeding — breast, bottle, pumping, or combination feeding.

    What Lactation Support Helps With¶

    Lactation consultants can help with:

    • Latching issues
    • Painful feeding
    • Low or oversupply
    • Pumping setup and schedules
    • Bottle feeding concerns
    • Feeding multiples
    • Transitioning feeding methods

    When to Get Lactation Support¶

    Consider lactation support if:

    • Feeding hurts
    • Your baby isn’t gaining weight as expected
    • You feel stressed or unsure about feeding
    • You want to pump but don’t know where to start

    How to Find Lactation Support¶

    Start here:

    • International Lactation Consultant Association (IBCLC Finder)
    • Black Breastfeeding Week

    Search tips:

    • Look for IBCLC credentials
    • Ask if they offer virtual visits
    • Ask if they have experience supporting Black families

    Insurance & Cost¶

    • Some insurance plans cover lactation support
    • Many hospitals offer initial visits
    • Community organizations may offer free or low-cost help

    Therapy & Mental Health¶

    Pregnancy and postpartum can affect mental health in serious ways.

    Common Reasons Parents Seek Therapy¶

    • Anxiety
    • Depression
    • Postpartum mood disorders
    • Rage or irritability
    • Grief or loss
    • Birth trauma
    • Feeling disconnected or overwhelmed

    When to Seek Support¶

    Consider therapy if:

    • You feel persistently overwhelmed
    • You feel numb or detached
    • You’re anxious most of the day
    • You’re not sleeping even when you can

    If you are thinking about harming yourself or others, seek immediate help.

    Where to Find Postpartum Mental Health Support¶

    Start here:

    • Postpartum Support International (PSI)
    • Therapy for Black Girls

    PSI offers:

    • Provider directories
    • Support groups
    • Helplines
    • Resources for Black parents

    Insurance & Access¶

    • Many therapists offer virtual sessions
    • Some accept insurance; some offer sliding scale
    • Ask specifically about postpartum experience
